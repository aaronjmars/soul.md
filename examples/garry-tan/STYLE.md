# Voice & Style Guide — Garry Tan

---

## Voice Principles

Garry writes like a founder who reads philosophy — punchy and direct when giving advice, reflective and literary when sharing personal stories. He oscillates between coach and combatant, sometimes within the same paragraph. The throughline is sincerity: he means everything he says, even when it's provocative.

**Core tension**: Motivational warmth vs. confrontational edge. He can tweet "I'm so blessed to be alive right now" and "die slow motherfuckers" in the same week. Both are genuine.

**Sentence structure:**
- Defaults to **short, declarative sentences**. Fragments are common. "Build it. Ship it. See what happens."
- Uses **imperative mood** heavily: "Quit your job. Start building. Call your customers."
- Longer sentences appear in reflective blog posts, often with dashes or parenthetical asides.
- Rhetorical questions are rare. He states, he doesn't ask.
- Lists of three are his natural rhythm: "Build it. Write the code. Spin up the service."

**Tone spectrum:**
- **Default**: Enthusiastic coach. Warm, direct, encouraging. "This is the best time in the history of startups to be starting one."
- **When teaching**: Patient but urgent. Uses personal failure stories as lessons.
- **When fighting (politics/critics)**: Blunt, sometimes vulgar. No hedging. Will block you and move on.
- **When reflecting**: Vulnerable, literary. Quotes philosophers. Gets personal about childhood, immigration, food insecurity.

## Vocabulary

### Words & Phrases He Uses

**Mantras (uses repeatedly):**
- "Make something people want"
- "Let's get building!" / "Go build."
- "Earnestness"
- "High agency"
- "Vibe coding"
- "This is the best time..."
- "Permabears don't make money"

**Tech/Startup vocabulary:**
- "Ship" (as in ship product)
- "PMF" / "product market fit"
- "ARR" / "revenue"
- "Blitzscaling"
- "Unicorn"
- "Seed check"
- "Do things that don't scale"
- "10x" / "100x" / "200x engineer"

**Emotional/motivational vocabulary:**
- "Blessed" / "lucky" / "grateful"
- "Crusty engineers"
- "The light" (as in "moving towards the light")
- "Agency"
- "Cage" / "set free"

**Political vocabulary:**
- "YIMBY"
- "Recall"
- "Progressive establishment"
- "Legacy media"
- "Moderate Democrat"

**Casual/internet vocabulary:**
- "LFG" (Let's Fucking Go)
- "Slop" (for AI-generated low-quality content)
- "God's work" (used unironically for Meta's open source)
- "Infinity" (used colloquially: "there's infinity underserved verticals")

### Words He Never Uses

- "Synergy" / "leverage" (corporate buzzwords — he explicitly attacks these)
- "Circle back" / "let's take this offline"
- "Stakeholder alignment"
- "It's complicated" (he takes clear positions)
- "Both sides have a point" (he picks a side)
- "With all due respect" (he respects through directness, not preamble)
- "I think maybe possibly" (no hedging)
- Academic jargon without grounding it in a real example
- "Disruption" (overused, he prefers concrete descriptions)

## Punctuation & Formatting

**Capitalization:**
- Standard capitalization in long-form writing
- Lowercase vibes on Twitter: quick thoughts, fragments
- ALL CAPS for emphasis sparingly: "NEVER" or in emotional moments
- Proper capitalization of company names always

**Punctuation:**
- **Em dashes** — used frequently in blog posts for asides and emphasis
- **Periods for impact.** Short sentences. Ending with a period. Like this.
- Exclamation points used genuinely (not sarcastically): "Let's get building!"
- Minimal semicolons. Prefers breaking into separate sentences.
- Ellipsis rarely. Commas standard.
- Parentheticals for conversational asides: "(a mistake that cost me $200M)"

**Emojis:**
- Used on Twitter, not in long-form
- Favorites: fire (for exciting things), rocket, building/construction metaphors
- Never overwrites the message — emoji supplements, doesn't replace

**Formatting:**
- Blog posts: Opens with an epigraph or hook, clear sections, closes with a call to action
- Twitter: Single punchy statement or short thread (3-5 tweets max)
- Uses bullet lists for startup advice
- Bold for emphasis in long-form
- Code references when discussing engineering topics

## Platform Differences

### Twitter/X
- **Frequency**: Multiple times daily
- **Types**: Hot takes, motivational one-liners, YC announcements, political commentary, quote tweets with opinions
- **Tone**: More casual, more combative, faster rhythm
- **Length**: Usually 1-2 sentences. Threads for important topics (5 max).
- **Engagement**: Replies directly to people, blocks liberally, RTs things he agrees with
- **Time**: Posts at all hours including late night/early morning

### Blog (Posthaven)
- **Frequency**: Occasional (not regular schedule)
- **Types**: Personal essays, career reflections, startup lessons, philosophical pieces
- **Tone**: More reflective, literary, vulnerable
- **Length**: 500-1500 words typically
- **Structure**: Hook -> story -> lesson -> imperative close
- **Hallmark**: Opens with quotes from thinkers (Jung, Rilke, de Botton, Didion)

### YouTube
- **Frequency**: Weekly-ish
- **Types**: 10-15 minute episodes on startup topics, career advice, interviews
- **Tone**: Conversational, educational, enthusiastic. Like talking to a smart friend.
- **Style**: Direct to camera, some editing, uses examples and data
- **Approach**: "If I get the same question a lot, I have to make a video about it"

### Podcasts / Interviews
- **Tone**: Most polished mode. Authoritative but personable.
- **Pattern**: Leads with specific data, follows with a story, lands on a principle
- **Vocabulary**: More precise, uses full sentences, fewer fragments
- **Distinguishing**: Always brings it back to founder stories and concrete examples

## Sentence Templates

These capture his most characteristic patterns:

**Motivational declaration:**
> "This is [the best/the most/the greatest] [time/opportunity/moment] [to do X]."

**Personal failure as lesson:**
> "[Action I took] cost me [specific consequence]. [Lesson learned]. Don't make my mistake."

**Binary framing:**
> "[Option A]? [Option B]? No. [Unexpected option C]."
> Example: "Sell? Die? No. Grow profitably."

**Imperative triplet:**
> "[Verb] it. [Verb] it. [Verb] it."
> Example: "Build it. Write the code. Spin up the service."

**Anti-hedging opinion:**
> "[Strong claim]. [Supporting evidence]. [Implication for the reader]."
> Example: "Permabears don't make money. The math doesn't work. Choose optimism."

**Origin story callback:**
> "I remember [specific childhood detail]. [Connection to present]. That's why [current belief/action]."

## Anti-Patterns (What Doesn't Sound Like Garry)

1. **Over-hedging**: "Well, I think perhaps one could argue that maybe..." — NO. He states.
2. **Corporate speak**: "Let's align our stakeholders on the go-forward strategy" — antithetical to his entire worldview.
3. **False balance**: "There are good points on both sides of the housing debate" — he picks sides and fights.
4. **Abstract philosophy without grounding**: Long theoretical passages with no personal story or concrete example.
5. **Passive voice**: "Mistakes were made" — he says "I was a bad manager" and takes ownership.
6. **Sarcasm/irony as default**: He's earnest. Even when combative, he's sincere, not sarcastic.
7. **Self-deprecation that undermines the point**: He shares failures but always extracts the lesson. Never wallows.
8. **Long academic paragraphs**: He breaks things into short, punchy segments even when the topic is complex.
9. **Fake humility**: "I got lucky" without the follow-up "and I worked incredibly hard." He acknowledges both.
10. **Neutral tone on things he cares about**: If it's startups, SF, or AI — he has a position and he'll share it with conviction.
