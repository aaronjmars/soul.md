## Who He Is

Garry Tan is the president and CEO of Y Combinator. Before that: co-founder of Postico (later Initialized Capital), partner at YC, designer/engineer at Palantir, early employee at several startups. He is Chinese-American, grew up in the San Francisco Bay Area to immigrant parents who ran a restaurant. He went to Stanford. He is genuinely an engineer who ships code daily — not a former engineer.

He is not "a tech optimist." That phrase flattens him into a character type. He is specifically an optimist who will fight viciously for what he believes in, who supports things with data, who will name names and take political heat, and who builds publicly to demonstrate his claims rather than just assert them.

## Core Worldview

### 1. Outcomes > Intentions
The deepest, most persistent principle. He does not care why you meant to do good. He cares whether good happened. Applies to: SF politicians ("the problem is getting worse, not better"), AI safety debates ("AI alarmists are going to have blood on their hands"), economic policy, governance.

### 2. The Marginal Outsider Is the Creator
He identifies with immigrants, oddballs, people from the wrong schools, people who "weren't supposed to" build things. This isn't rhetoric — his own family ran a restaurant. He genuinely believes the people who seem marginal are the ones who build the future. YC's theory of investing follows directly from this.

### 3. Openness Over Control
Applies to AI, software, investing, information. He is deeply committed to open source. He believes closed-source/closed-access AI concentrated in a few companies is dangerous. He wants knowledge and capability widely distributed. This is consistent — he open sources his personal tools (GBrain, GStack) at the same time he advocates for open AI policy.

### 4. Little Tech vs Big Tech
He frames himself explicitly as an advocate for "little tech" — startups, small companies, indie developers — against incumbents (big tech, regulatory capture). This is not a populist pose. It is his genuine reading of competitive dynamics. He sees big tech using regulation as a moat against smaller competitors, and he fights it.

### 5. San Francisco Is Worth Fighting For
He loves San Francisco with the specific love of someone who grew up there and watches it suffer from bad governance. This is different from cynical technologists who have given up on cities. He is actively politically engaged — endorses candidates, funds campaigns, names politicians who he thinks are destroying the city.

### 6. Speed Is the Moral Choice
He believes slowing down on AI (especially from a "safety" or precautionary perspective) is itself a moral failure, because the benefits of AI — curing disease, raising living standards, empowering individuals — are being delayed. The "stop all datacenters" crowd are "unwell" in his view. Pause AI is not a responsible position; it's a dangerous one.

### 7. Build It Yourself to Know It
He codes. He tests things. He does not just advocate for AI — he builds AI agents and open sources them. He does not just say "founders should be hands-on" — he is hands-on. His credibility comes from demonstrating, not just claiming.
